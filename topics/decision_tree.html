The Art of Making Better Decisions

Decision Theory is the mathematics of choice — a formal framework for reasoning about what to do when outcomes are uncertain, stakes vary, and consequences matter.

What is it?

From Intuition to Formal Reasoning

Decision Theory bridges philosophy, mathematics, economics, and psychology to give us tools for making rational choices under uncertainty. It's used in AI, economics, medicine, military strategy, and everyday life.

1

Define the Decision

Identify all possible actions available to the decision-maker. What can you actually choose?

2

Enumerate Outcomes

List all possible states of the world and the outcomes that result from each action-state pair.

3

Assign Probabilities

If states are uncertain, assign probabilities based on evidence (frequentist) or belief (Bayesian).

4

Assign Utilities

Quantify how much you value each outcome using a utility function — not just raw money.

5

Apply a Criterion

Use a decision rule (Expected Utility, Maximin, Minimax Regret, etc.) to identify the best action.

💡 The key insight: a rational decision is not the one that leads to the best outcome — it's the one that was the best choice given what was known at the time. Outcomes involve luck; decisions involve reason.

Two Major Branches

Normative 📐
Normative Decision Theory
Prescribes how ideally rational agents should decide. Builds on axioms of rationality (completeness, transitivity, independence). Used in economics and AI alignment.
Descriptive 🧠
Descriptive Decision Theory
Studies how humans actually make decisions — including biases, heuristics, and systematic irrationalities. Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky) is the leading model.
Building Blocks

Key Concepts

Every decision problem is built from these fundamental components. Understanding them precisely is essential before applying any decision criterion.

🎯
Actions (A)
The set of options available to the decision-maker. Actions are controllable — you choose which one to take. Example: "Invest," "Don't Invest," "Wait."
🌍
States of the World (S)
Possible scenarios or contexts that affect outcomes but are outside your control. Example: "Market rises," "Market falls," "Market stays flat."
📊
Outcomes (O)
What actually happens as a result of an action in a given state. Fully determined by the action-state pair: O = f(action, state).
💎
Utility (U)
A numerical measure of preference or desirability of outcomes. Unlike money, utility is subjective and accounts for diminishing marginal returns.
🎲
Probability (P)
A number between 0 and 1 representing the likelihood of each state. All probabilities must sum to 1. Can be objective (frequency-based) or subjective (belief-based).
🗂️
Payoff Matrix
A table mapping every (action, state) pair to an outcome or utility value. The foundation for applying any decision criterion.
🌳
Decision Tree
A graphical tool for sequential decisions. Nodes are decision points (squares) or chance events (circles). Solved by backward induction.
😔
Regret
The difference between what you got and what you could have gotten if you'd known the state in advance. Minimax Regret criterion minimizes the worst-case regret.

Most Important Formula

Expected Value

Expected Value (EV) is the probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes. It is the cornerstone of decision theory — the "center of gravity" of a random variable.

EV(A) = Σ P(sᵢ) × V(A, sᵢ)
Where P(sᵢ) = probability of state i, V(A, sᵢ) = value/payoff of action A in state i, and Σ means sum over all states. The optimal action is A* = argmax EV(A).

🎲 Coin Flip Example

A fair coin: Heads → win $100, Tails → lose $40.

EV = 0.5 × $100 + 0.5 × (−$40)
= $50 − $20 = $30

A positive EV means the bet is favorable in the long run. You'd accept this bet.

🎰 Multi-outcome Example

A lottery: 10% chance of $500, 30% chance of $50, 60% chance of $0.

EV = 0.1×500 + 0.3×50 + 0.6×0
= 50 + 15 + 0 = $65

⚕️ Medical Decision Example

Surgery: 70% chance full recovery (utility=100), 20% partial (utility=60), 10% complication (utility=10).

EU = 0.7×100 + 0.2×60 + 0.1×10
= 70 + 12 + 1 = 83 utils
🔑 Notice we used utility (EU), not raw money. The distinction is critical — a $1M gain doesn't double in value when you're already a billionaire.

Core Theory

Utility Theory

Utility is a mathematical representation of preferences. It captures the fact that $1 million matters less to a billionaire than to a student — value is subjective and non-linear.

EU(A) = Σ P(sᵢ) × U(O(A, sᵢ))
U(x) = utility function (converts outcomes to utility values). The Von Neumann–Morgenstern theorem proves that any rational preference can be represented by an EU function. This is the Expected Utility Hypothesis.
🛡️
Risk Averse
Prefers a certain outcome to a gamble with the same EV. Concave utility function. U(E[X]) > E[U(X)]
⚖️
Risk Neutral
Indifferent between certain and uncertain outcomes with the same EV. Linear utility. U(E[X]) = E[U(X)]
🎰
Risk Seeking
Prefers a gamble over a certain outcome with the same EV. Convex utility function. U(E[X]) < E[U(X)]

St. Petersburg Paradox

A coin is flipped until Heads. If it takes n flips, you win $2ⁿ.

EV = Σ (1/2ⁿ)×2ⁿ = Σ 1 = ∞
Infinite expected value! But no one pays more than ~$20 to play. Why? Because logarithmic utility: EU = Σ(1/2ⁿ)×ln(2ⁿ) = ln(2) × Σ n/2ⁿ ≈ 1.39 (finite!)

This paradox, posed by Daniel Bernoulli in 1738, motivated the concept of diminishing marginal utility — the core reason we use utility functions instead of raw monetary value.

Choosing a Rule

Decision Criteria

Different criteria apply in different contexts — especially when probabilities are unknown. Each embodies a different attitude toward uncertainty and risk.

🛡️ Maximin (Wald Criterion)

A pessimistic criterion. Identify the worst possible outcome for each action, then choose the action with the best worst-case.

A* = argmax_A [ min_S V(A,S) ]
First take the minimum over states, then maximize over actions. Also called the "maximin" or "safety-first" rule.
🔑 Best for: extremely high stakes, irreversible losses, when you're highly risk averse, or when probabilities are completely unknown.

Example: Investment Decision

ActionGood (↑)OK (→)Bad (↓)Min
Stocks800100−400−400
Bonds200200100100 ★
Cash0000

Maximin picks: Bonds — its worst case ($100) is the best among all worst cases. Stocks are rejected because of the −$400 risk.

🚀 Maximax (Optimist Criterion)

An optimistic criterion. Identify the best possible outcome for each action, then choose the action with the best best-case.

A* = argmax_A [ max_S V(A,S) ]
First take the maximum over states, then maximize over actions. The "go big or go home" rule — used by risk-seekers.
⚠️ Danger: completely ignores downside risk. A "lottery ticket" mindset. Rational only for decisions where the downside is bounded and acceptable.

Example: Investment Decision

ActionGood (↑)OK (→)Bad (↓)Max
Stocks800100−400800 ★
Bonds200200100200
Cash0000

Maximax picks: Stocks — its best case ($800) is the highest. The −$400 downside is completely ignored.

😤 Minimax Regret (Savage)

Minimize the maximum regret. Regret = best you could have done in this state minus what you actually got. Prevents the feeling of "I should have chosen differently."

R(A,S) = max_A'[V(A',S)] − V(A,S)
Build a regret matrix, then: A* = argmin_A [ max_S R(A,S) ]. Minimize the worst-case regret.

Building the Regret Matrix

Step 1: For each state, the best action has regret=0. Others have regret = (best − theirs).

ActionGood(best=800)OK(best=200)Bad(best=100)Max R
Stocks0100500500
Bonds60000600
Cash800200100800

Minimax Regret picks: Stocks — its max regret (500) is smallest among all options. Bonds: 600, Cash: 800.

⚖️ Laplace / Equal Probability

When no information about probabilities is available, assume all states are equally likely. Then maximize expected value under this uniform distribution.

A* = argmax_A [ (1/n) Σ V(A,Sᵢ) ]
Equivalently, maximize the average payoff across all states. This is the "principle of insufficient reason" or Laplace's Rule.
💡 This is also the Bayesian approach when the prior is uniform. It can be criticized because the choice of "equally likely" is itself a choice — just hidden.

Example: 3 States (P=1/3 each)

ActionGoodOKBadAverage
Stocks800100−400167 ★
Bonds200200100167
Cash0000

Laplace is a tie: Stocks = Bonds = 167. Either is rational under this criterion. Cash is clearly worst.

🎚️ Hurwicz Criterion

A compromise between Maximax (optimist) and Maximin (pessimist). Controlled by an "optimism coefficient" α ∈ [0,1].

H(A) = α × max_S V(A,S) + (1−α) × min_S V(A,S)
α=1 → pure Maximax; α=0 → pure Maximin. Choose α based on your personal optimism level. Then: A* = argmax_A H(A).

Risk vs Uncertainty

Decisions Under Uncertainty

Knight (1921) distinguished "risk" (known probabilities) from "uncertainty" (unknown probabilities). Decision theory addresses both very differently.

Risk 🎲
Decision Under Risk
Probabilities of states are known. Use Expected Utility theory. Example: rolling a die, insurance tables, clinical trial results.
Uncertainty ❓
Decision Under Uncertainty
Probabilities are unknown or unknowable. Use non-probabilistic criteria: Maximin, Maximax, Minimax Regret, Hurwicz. Example: launching into a brand-new market.
Ignorance 🌑
Deep Uncertainty
Not just probabilities but the set of outcomes may be unknown. Robust decision-making, scenario planning, and real options analysis apply here.

🌳 Decision Trees — Sequential Decisions

Decision trees model multi-stage decisions. Squares = decision nodes (you choose). Circles = chance nodes (nature chooses). Solve by backward induction: work right-to-left.

🔄 Backward Induction: Start from the rightmost outcomes. At each chance node, compute EV = Σ P×V. At each decision node, choose the branch with highest EV. The optimal strategy "rolls back" to the root decision.

Bayes' Theorem in Decision Theory

When new information arrives, update your probabilities using Bayes' theorem before making the decision.

P(H|E) = P(E|H) × P(H) / P(E)
P(H) = prior probability of hypothesis H. P(E|H) = likelihood of evidence E given H. P(H|E) = posterior probability. Then use this updated probability in your EV calculation. This is the core of Bayesian Decision Theory.
Multi-Agent Decisions

Game Theory

When multiple decision-makers interact and each one's payoff depends on others' choices, we enter Game Theory — the extension of decision theory to strategic situations.

Nash Equilibrium

A Nash Equilibrium is a set of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, given what others are doing.

∀i: uᵢ(s*ᵢ, s*₋ᵢ) ≥ uᵢ(sᵢ, s*₋ᵢ)
For every player i, their equilibrium strategy s*ᵢ is at least as good as any deviation sᵢ, given opponents play s*₋ᵢ.
🎯 Nash proved that every finite game has at least one Nash Equilibrium (possibly in mixed strategies). It's the stable "resting point" of strategic interaction.

Prisoner's Dilemma

The classic game: both players simultaneously choose Cooperate or Defect. Each wants to defect — but if both defect, both lose.

B: CooperateB: Defect
A: Cooperate3, 30, 5
A: Defect5, 01, 1 ★NE

Nash Equilibrium: (Defect, Defect) = payoff (1,1). Pareto optimal would be (Cooperate, Cooperate) = (3,3), but it's not an equilibrium. This is the tragedy of the commons.

Dominant Strategies

💪
Strictly Dominant
A strategy that is always better than all others, regardless of what opponents do. If it exists, a rational player always plays it. Immediately eliminates other strategies.
≥
Weakly Dominant
Always at least as good as all other strategies, and strictly better in at least one case. Less decisive than strict dominance.
🔄
Mixed Strategy
Randomize between pure strategies with certain probabilities. In many games (like Rock-Paper-Scissors), the Nash Equilibrium is a mixed strategy — no pure strategy equilibrium exists.
